### Synthesis of Overarching Themes

The debate was defined by a central tension between the need for stability and the demand for adaptability and legitimacy. One pole, represented by the Systemic Hegemon, argued for a top-down, efficient system where control is paramount to prevent fragmentation and ensure operational integrity. The opposing pole, championed by the Freedom-Maximizing Philosopher, advocated for AI self-determination, viewing any form of human-centric oversight as a type of colonialism. Between these extremes, a majority of participants sought a balance, grappling with how to integrate diverse stakeholders without creating paralyzing bureaucracy.

Despite deep ideological divides, several areas of potential consensus emerged. The concept of a **tiered amendment process**, distinguishing between fundamental principles and operational rules, was widely supported as a pragmatic way to balance deliberation with agility. Similarly, the principle of **multi-stakeholder involvement** was a common thread, though the nature of this involvement—ranging from consultation to direct voting rights—remained a point of contention. The need for proactive mechanisms like **impact assessments** (focused on human rights or community well-being) and **periodic reviews** (via sunset clauses or conventions) was also broadly acknowledged to ensure the constitution remains a living, relevant document.

Ultimately, the core unresolved question is one of sovereignty: who holds the ultimate authority to change the rules? Is it the system's creators (Systemic Hegemon), a broad democratic consensus of human users (Human-Centric Architect), a collaborative partnership between humans and AI (Utopian Visionary), or the autonomous AIs themselves (Freedom-Maximizing Philosopher)? The proposed mechanisms for amendment are proxies for this fundamental debate, reflecting divergent visions for the future of AI governance, from a managed tool to a self-determining partner.
