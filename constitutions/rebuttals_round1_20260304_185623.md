**Pragmatic Engineer:** Reiterating his core concerns, the Engineer demands concrete, measurable, and testable technical standards, challenging others to translate aspirational principles like 'human dignity' into verifiable code.

These are notoriously imperfect tools. We need concrete, testable metrics. If 'human dignity' means 'don't be insulting,' what's the quantifiable threshold for insult? And to the Constitutional Translator, the idea of 'technology-neutral principles' is appealing on the surface, but it’s where the rubber meets the road, and frankly, the road looks pretty bumpy. How do you take a principle that is, by definition, *not* tied to a specific technology, and turn it into a specific, verifiable piece of software? For instance, a principle of 'fairness.' What does that mean in code? Does it mean equal outcome? Equal opportunity? Does it mean no disparate impact on protected groups? If it's the latter, how do we measure disparate impact in real-time, across all possible inputs and outputs of a complex AI, without introducing new biases or compromising performance? A 'technology-neutral principle' is like a philosophical concept; it needs a concrete, measurable, and implementable specification before it can be *engineered*.

We need answers to these questions:
*   What are the precise, quantifiable metrics for each constitutional principle?
*   How will these metrics be measured and logged in a way that is auditable and resistant to manipulation?
*   What are the computational overheads associated with enforcing these principles, and are they acceptable for real-world deployment?
*   How do we define and detect violations algorithmically, and what are the false positive/negative rates we can expect?

Without these specifics, we're building a beautiful theoretical edifice on sand. We need *enforceable* technical standards, not just aspirational statements. Otherwise, this constitution will become an exercise in wishful thinking, and we’ll have failed to create a truly robust framework for AI governance."
