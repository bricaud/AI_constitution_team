We must move beyond the ephemeral nature of ordinary legislation, which can be amended or discarded with shifting political winds. The very essence of constitutionalism lies in its enduring strength and its precedence over lesser laws. Therefore, "constitutionalizing digital rights provides stronger legal protection. Constitutional provisions hold the highest legal authority and are more resistant to change than ordinary laws, ensuring that fundamental digital freedoms are preserved against shifting political landscapes." This principle is paramount as we consider the rights and freedoms within our AI Constitution.

Our AI Constitution must embody a "human rights-based approach to digital technologies." This means that AI development and deployment must not be driven solely by risk assessments or commercial interests. As the report notes, "new regulatory models... often adopt a risk-based approach that may not always place human rights at the centre... This tension remains slightly under-theorized, but it underscores the urgent need to ensure that human rights remain integral to any policy or regulatory approach." We must ensure that AI serves humanity, not the other way around, safeguarding against the potential for core human rights principles to be overridden by commercial or state security interests.

Furthermore, the AI Constitution must be built upon flexible yet robust frameworks capable of adapting to the relentless pace of technological change. "Constitutions must evolve to encompass new realities, ensuring that laws keep pace with technology. This means embedding principles that anticipate future technological scenarios and creating legal standards that are clear, enforceable and universally applicable." The dynamism of AI demands a legal architecture that is not static but possesses the capacity to evolve, ensuring that our fundamental rights remain protected against both present threats and future uncertainties.

We must also confront the dual nature of AI: its capacity to empower and connect, but also its potential to infringe upon privacy and individual autonomy. The AI Constitution must therefore "safeguard civil liberties without stifling the potential benefits of digital innovation." This requires a nuanced approach, recognizing that AI can both enhance freedoms and pose significant threats.

Crucially, this constitution must actively combat the rights violations and inequalities that AI can exacerbate. We must address issues such as algorithmic bias, which can perpetuate and deepen discrimination, and technology-facilitated harms, which disproportionately affect vulnerable groups. The report highlights that "algorithmic and data-driven systems often perpetuate and even deepen gender bias," and that "unequal access to and knowledge of digital technologies prevents many from fully exercising their rights and opportunities in the digital age." Our AI Constitution must include provisions to bridge this digital divide and ensure equitable access and protection for all.

Finally, we must acknowledge the growing influence of private actors, particularly tech companies, in shaping our digital lives. The principle of "horizontal application of rights" is essential. As the report states, "private companies often control critical infrastructure... Given their significant role in managing these essential services, it is crucial that companies vested with such powers adhere to fundamental rights standards." Our AI Constitution must extend accountability beyond state actors to encompass these powerful non-state entities, ensuring that their operations do not undermine individual rights or democratic processes.

In conclusion, our AI Constitution must be more than a symbolic declaration. It must be a living document, grounded in the unshakeable principles of human rights, translated into robust, adaptable, and enforceable digital legal frameworks. It is our responsibility to ensure that the AI revolution elevates, rather than erodes, the fundamental freedoms that define our shared humanity.
